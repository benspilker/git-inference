Tie-dye was not "invented" at a single point in time—it's an ancient resist-dyeing technique that developed independently in multiple cultures.⁠Nationalgeographic

The oldest known form is bandhani from the Indus Valley Civilization (modern-day northern India/Pakistan), dating back to around 4000 B.C. (over 6,000 years ago). It involved tying small knots in fabric before dyeing, often for ceremonial use.⁠Nationalgeographic

Other early examples include:

China (Sui dynasty, ~5th century AD; some references note 6th century or earlier "zha ran").
Japan (shibori technique, from the 8th century Nara period).
Pre-Columbian Peru (amarra style, surviving examples from ~500–810 AD).⁠Wikipedia

Similar methods appeared in West Africa, Southeast Asia, and elsewhere, likely as practical "happy accidents" during dyeing. Many early textiles haven't survived due to their organic nature.

The modern Western/psychedelic tie-dye style (bright swirls on T-shirts) became popular in the U.S. in the late 1960s counterculture, influenced by musicians like Janis Joplin—not the origin of the technique itself.⁠Wikipedia

In short: ancient roots in India (~4000 B.C.), with parallel traditions worldwide; 1960s popularized the casual version we know today.
