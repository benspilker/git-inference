Authentic Neapolitan pizza dough (AVPN-style, adapted for home use) uses only four ingredients: Tipo 00 flour (preferred, or strong bread flour), water, sea salt, and a tiny amount of yeast. No oil, sugar, or additives. This yields 4 dough balls (~250–270 g each) for 12–13 inch pizzas.

Ingredients (for 4 pizzas)
Water: 370 g (room temperature, ~20–24°C; ~60% hydration for good stretch and home-oven results)
Fine sea salt: 18–20 g (about 3%)
Instant dry yeast (or active dry): 0.3–0.5 g (very little for slow fermentation; or 1–1.5 g fresh yeast)
Tipo 00 pizza flour (Caputo Pizzeria or equivalent): 620 g (plus a little extra for dusting)

Note: Scale up/down proportionally. For colder kitchens or longer ferments, drop yeast to the lower end.

Step-by-Step Instructions
Dissolve salt: In a large bowl or mixer, add the water and fully dissolve the salt. (Do this first.)
Add yeast safely: Sprinkle in the yeast and stir to dissolve. Immediately add ~10–15% of the flour (about 60–90 g) and mix into a loose slurry. This prevents direct salt-yeast contact longer than 5 minutes, which can kill yeast cells.
Mix the dough: Gradually add the remaining flour while mixing by hand (or low speed on a stand mixer with dough hook). Stop when it forms a shaggy mass—no dry flour left. Cover and rest 20–30 minutes (autolyse). This hydrates the flour fully and makes kneading easier.
Knead: Turn onto a lightly floured counter. Knead vigorously by hand for 10–15 minutes (or 8–10 minutes in mixer on medium) until the dough is smooth, elastic, and passes the windowpane test (stretch a small piece thin enough to see light through without tearing). It should feel silky and spring back slowly. If it feels sticky at first, resist adding flour—hydration is key.
Bulk fermentation (first rise): Form into a smooth ball, place in a lightly oiled bowl, cover tightly (plastic wrap or lid), and let rest at room temperature (22–26°C ideal).
Divide and shape balls: After bulk rise, gently turn dough out. Divide into 4 equal portions (~250–270 g each). Shape each into a tight ball: cup your hand and roll in gentle circles on the counter (no tearing). Place seam-side down in a lightly oiled proofing box, tray, or individual containers. Cover.
Final proof: See fermentation options below. Dough is ready when it has increased ~50–100% in size, feels airy, and a gentle finger press leaves a slow rebound (not collapsing or springing back instantly).
Use: Remove from proofing container carefully. On a lightly floured (or semolina-dusted) surface, press and stretch by hand into a 12–13 inch round with a thin center and slightly thicker ½–¾ inch cornicione (rim). Do not use a rolling pin. Top minimally and bake immediately in the hottest oven possible (ideally 450–500°C wood-fired; home oven 480–500°F / 250–260°C with pizza steel/stone for 60–90 seconds per side, turning as needed).
Fermentation Options & Timing

Traditional room-temperature (fastest, classic AVPN-style):

Bulk: 1–2 hours (until ~50% rise).
Ball proof: 4–8 hours at 22–26°C.
Total: 6–10 hours. Best flavor develops around 8 hours.

Long room-temperature (maximum flavor):

Bulk: 2 hours.
Ball proof: 18–24 hours at cooler room temp (~18–22°C). Monitor to avoid overproofing.

Cold fermentation (best for home schedules and flavor):

After kneading, bulk rest 30–60 minutes at room temp.
Divide into balls and refrigerate immediately (covered) for 24–48 hours (up to 72 hours max).
2–4 hours before baking: remove to room temp to finish proofing.
This develops complex flavor and is forgiving.

Yeast calculator tip: At warmer temps (>26°C), reduce yeast slightly to avoid overproofing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
Too much yeast → fast, bland rise and poor structure. Stick to tiny amounts for slow fermentation.
Direct salt-yeast contact → weakened yeast and flat dough. Always buffer with flour/water.
Under- or over-kneading → dough tears when stretching or lacks air pockets. Use windowpane test.
Wrong water temperature → hot water kills yeast; ice-cold slows everything. Room temp is best.
Over-flouring the bench during shaping → dry, tough crust. Use minimal flour or semolina only under the dough.
Rough handling of balls → deflates the air pockets needed for that signature puffy cornicione. Be gentle.
Overproofing → dough collapses, tastes boozy, and stretches poorly. Underproofing → tight and hard to stretch.
Using the wrong flour → all-purpose lacks gluten strength; Tipo 00 or high-protein bread flour is essential.
Adding oil/sugar → not authentic Neapolitan and changes texture.

This dough rewards patience—long, slow fermentation is what gives Neapolitan pizza its light, digestible, bubbly crust. Make it the day before for best results. Once you nail the dough, the rest is simple topping and high heat. Enjoy!
