Leaves are usually green because of chlorophyll, the pigment that absorbs red and blue light for photosynthesis and reflects green wavelengths. However, in many trees—especially during blooming or early leaf growth—you may see leaves that are red, purple, yellow, or even almost white. This happens for several biological reasons:

1. Different pigments are present

Leaves don’t rely on chlorophyll alone. They also contain other pigments:

anthocyanins
These create red, purple, or bronze colors. They are common in young leaves and in some ornamental trees (like red maples or plum trees).

carotenoids
These produce yellow, orange, or gold tones. They’re always present but usually masked by chlorophyll.

When chlorophyll is low or not yet fully developed, these pigments become visible.

2. Young leaves haven’t fully developed chlorophyll

During early spring (around bloom time), newly emerging leaves are still immature. At this stage:

Chlorophyll production is still ramping up

Protective pigments (like anthocyanins) are more dominant

This is why many trees have reddish or bronze-tinted new growth that later turns green.

3. Protection from light and environmental stress

Anthocyanins in particular act like a natural “sunscreen”:

They protect young leaves from intense sunlight and UV radiation

They reduce damage from cold temperatures or sudden environmental changes

They help prevent oxidative stress while the leaf’s photosynthetic system is still developing

So those red or purple tones are actually a protective strategy, not just decoration.

4. Genetic traits in certain species

Some trees are genetically programmed to have non-green leaves, even when mature. For example:

Ornamental maples or plum trees maintain deep red or purple leaves

Some species have variegated leaves (green mixed with white or yellow)

In these cases, chlorophyll is either reduced or unevenly distributed, allowing other pigments to dominate.

5. Seasonal transitions

Even outside of autumn, pigment balance can shift:

In early growth → reds and bronzes dominate

In peak growth → chlorophyll dominates (green)

In fall → chlorophyll breaks down, revealing yellows, oranges, and reds again

6. Nutrient or environmental factors (less common during bloom)

Occasionally, unusual leaf colors can also come from:

Nutrient deficiencies

Soil pH issues

Stress (drought, disease, etc.)

But during blooming season, color differences are usually normal and healthy.

Bottom line

Leaves aren’t always green because chlorophyll isn’t always the dominant pigment. During bloom or early growth, other pigments—especially anthocyanins and carotenoids—take center stage, often serving protective roles until the leaf matures.
