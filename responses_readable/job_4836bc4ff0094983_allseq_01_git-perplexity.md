Here’s a solid, home-friendly Neapolitan pizza dough method.

Ingredients

For about 3–4 dough balls:

500 g tipo “00” flour, or strong bread flour if needed.

325 g water, room temperature.

12–13 g fine salt.

0.5–1 g instant yeast, or about 1.5–2 g active dry yeast.

A classic Neapolitan dough is very simple: flour, water, salt, and a tiny amount of yeast. Typical versions use long fermentation to build flavor instead of oil, sugar, or a lot of yeast.

Step by step

Dissolve the salt in the water.

Add about 10% of the flour and mix.

Add the yeast, then gradually add the remaining flour.

Mix until no dry flour remains.

Rest the dough 15–20 minutes.

Knead briefly until smooth and elastic, or use stretch-and-folds if you prefer gentler handling.

Cover and let it ferment.

Divide into dough balls.

Ball the dough tightly.

Let the balls proof until relaxed and airy.

Stretch, top lightly, and bake as hot as possible.

This kind of dough is usually mixed, briefly rested, then fermented slowly to develop extensibility and flavor.

Fermentation options
Option A: Same-day-ish room temp plus cold rest

Bulk ferment at room temperature for about 12–20 hours.

Ball the dough.

Refrigerate for 24–48 hours.

Bring back to room temperature for about 3–4 hours before baking.

This is a common home approach and gives good flavor with manageable timing.

Option B: Long cold fermentation

Mix and lightly knead.

Let it sit 1–2 hours at room temperature.

Ball it.

Refrigerate for 24–72 hours.

Warm at room temperature before baking.

Longer fermentation generally increases flavor complexity and improves texture.

Option C: Room-temp focused

Use very little yeast.

Ferment mostly at room temperature for 8–12 hours.

This can work, but you need tighter control of yeast and room temperature so it doesn’t overproof.

Dough cues

Look for:

A smooth, extensible dough.

Noticeable rise, but not collapse.

Dough that feels airy and relaxed when shaped.

With slow-fermented dough, exact doubling is less important than good volume increase and a puffy, workable texture.

Common mistakes

Using too much yeast. Long fermentation needs very little yeast.

Adding oil or sugar if you want a traditional Neapolitan-style dough.

Baking in an oven that isn’t hot enough. Neapolitan pizza works best with very high heat.

Overkneading. Too much mixing can make the dough tight.

Overproofing. If it gets overly gassy, slack, and hard to shape, it has gone too far.

Under-resting before stretching. Cold dough will fight you and tear.

Practical example

If you want a reliable home schedule:

Evening 1: Mix dough.

Day 2: Ball the dough.

Day 3: Bake after a few hours at room temperature.

That gives you enough fermentation for flavor without making the process fussy.
