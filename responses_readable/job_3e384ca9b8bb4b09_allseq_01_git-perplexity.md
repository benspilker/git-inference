Here’s a reliable step-by-step method for Neapolitan pizza dough.

Ingredients

For about 4 dough balls:

500 g 00 flour

325 g water, room temperature

12–15 g fine salt

0.5–1 g instant yeast, or about 1–2 g fresh yeast

That gives roughly 65% hydration, which is a good home-kitchen starting point. If your kitchen is very warm or you want a more extensible dough, you can slightly increase water; if it’s very humid or you’re new to handling wet dough, stay closer to 62–63%.

Step-by-step method

Dissolve the salt in the water.

Add about 10% of the flour and mix briefly.

Add the yeast and stir to dissolve.

Add the remaining flour gradually and mix until no dry flour remains.

Knead until smooth and elastic, about 8–12 minutes by hand or 5–7 minutes in a mixer on low speed.

Cover and rest the dough 15–20 minutes.

Knead briefly again if needed until the surface is smooth.

Let it ferment in bulk, then divide into balls.

Ball the dough tightly and let the balls proof until relaxed and puffy.

Stretch gently, top lightly, and bake very hot.

Timing options
Same-day schedule

Mix and knead: 20–30 minutes total.

Bulk rest: 1–2 hours.

Divide and ball: after bulk rise begins, not necessarily doubled.

Final proof: 4–6 hours at room temperature.

Bake when the dough feels airy, soft, and extensible.

Overnight or long fermentation

Mix and knead: same as above.

Bulk rest: 30–60 minutes.

Ball the dough.

Refrigerate 12–48 hours.

Bring to room temperature 2–4 hours before baking.

Traditional room-temperature fermentation

Bulk ferment: about 8–16 hours at cool room temperature.

Balling may happen before or after the bulk phase depending on your style.

Final proof: 2–4 hours.

Longer fermentation usually improves flavor and stretch, but it also makes timing more temperature-sensitive.

How to know it’s ready

The dough looks smoother and slightly expanded.

It feels soft, lively, and elastic.

A finger poke springs back slowly, not instantly.

When stretched, it resists a little but doesn’t tear easily.

If it snaps back too much, it’s underproofed. If it collapses, becomes sticky, or smells sharply alcoholic, it’s likely overproofed.

Common mistakes to avoid

Using too much yeast. This makes the dough rise too fast and taste less developed.

Adding oil, sugar, or too much flour. Classic Neapolitan dough is simple and should stay lean.

Kneading too aggressively. You want smooth gluten development, not a dense dough.

Fermenting too warm. Heat can cause the dough to overproof and become weak.

Stretching with a rolling pin. That pushes out gas and ruins the open rim.

Using too much topping. Neapolitan pizza should be lightly topped so it bakes quickly.

Baking at too low a temperature. You want the hottest oven you can safely manage.

Practical baking notes

A true Neapolitan pizza bakes in a very hot wood-fired oven, but home ovens can still do well. Preheat a pizza stone or steel as hot as your oven allows for at least 45–60 minutes, then bake on the top rack or with broiler help if available.

A simple home version is:

Preheat at maximum temperature.

Stretch the dough by hand into a thin center and thicker rim.

Add sauce, a little cheese, and minimal toppings.

Bake until the crust has browned spots and the bottom is cooked through.
